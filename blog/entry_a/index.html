Every day we face decisions about small things and events that can change our lives. But, how do we choose between them? You probably never thought about this before. You shouldn’t, as it happens. For example while writing this article I’m having tea instead of coffee.

Why is this relevant? From a personal view, this might not be a problem. But, it matters when trying to understand human interactions and our lives in society. In doing so, we want to compare preferences across individuals. We also want to put numbers to those decisions. This can serve different purposes. For example, from a marketing view, it would be interesting to predict people’s choices. This is to design products that match those preferences and appeal to customers. Policymakers also want to understand individual preferences. This helps them design policies to maximize average utility across a population. Did you say utility?

The response to this question dates back to the 19th century. It comes from the work of moral philosophers. The most notable are Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill. In his famous book Utilitarianism, Mill’s states that:

“The doctrine that the basis of morals is utility, or the Greatest Happiness Principle, holds that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness.”

Utilitarians stand up for utility as the test of right and wrong. They say people should desire things that maximize their utility. The social sciences have developed and economists use utility theory to explain choices. They use it to measure satisfaction, which they call expected utility. Suggesting that instead of focusing on the objects of our decisions. The focus should be on how they make us feel. In this way every person has a unique valuation or utility function. For example, I prefer going for a run on a Sunday morning rather than staying at home. It gives me more utility. What would you prefer to do in that case?

If we keep asking that question we see that there are as many utility functions as people in the world.If that’s not enough, preferences change over time and adjust depending on the context. On top of that, there is the assumption of rationality. But, as we know, our short-term decisions can contradict our long-term goals. This shows our humanity. It can seem chaotic and lead to each person having a unique view of the world. But, history has shown we were able to cooperate and make social norms that allow us to live in society. Even people from different times and cultures share some preferences. After all, we are not as different as we thought.

Introducing AI

In the last few years, artificial intelligence (AI) has taken the world by storm. With the launch of Chat GPT and similar models, anyone with internet access can benefit from it. Industry leaders, experts, politicians, and the press talk about the future. They say AI can boost productivity and well-being like never before. But, the risks match the potential gains. In general, AI is the science to teach machines human-like abilities. As a consequence, as machines become more powerful, they will surpass us.

In the last few years, the focus has been on large language models. Examples include Chat GPT, Gemini, and PaLM. They can create content like humans. Originally, researchers trained these models to do specific tasks. For example, face recognition or playing Go. This is known as narrow AI, and on this topic, AI systems have outperformed the best humans. As happened in March 2016 when AlphaGo from DeepMind defeated a world champion, a decade before experts thought possible.

Where is AI going from here?

AI is advancing fast. It is evolving towards artificial general intelligence. That is the state in which machines can do all human activities. It is someone you can talk and can do anything not only one specific thing. We are not there yet, but machines will face decisions just as humans do every day. AI expert Stuart Russel on his book Human Compatible AI and the Problem of Control addresses this problem. He proposes that AI researchers, in thinking about how to create beneficial AI, must follow three principles.

  • The machine’s only objective is to maximize the realization of human preferences.
  • The machine is initially uncertain about what those preferences are.
  • The ultimate source of information about human preferences is human behavior.

It is interesting to observe how human preferences serve as the basis for these principles. They return to the moral philosopher’s dilemma adding the complexity that machines should learn to understand us. They should do this based on the observation of our choices (we do the same with the rest of the world). Then, they go back to the first principle. It sounds promising as long as we can understand human preferences.

Conclusion

To wrap up this article, we are entering the most revolutionary period ever. It will give us a unique chance to shape the world for the better. What to expect? It is unclear how and when AI will reach that point. But, progress will continue and speed up. As the saying goes, unprecedented times call for unprecedented actions. Academics, business, and international organisations are already working on making AI good for humans. We will need interdisciplinary cooperation to achieve this goal. The most important part is putting our humanity at the centre of decision-making. Humans have needed this balance throughout history. Making beneficial AI will need it too. It’s a balance between selfish and selfless.
